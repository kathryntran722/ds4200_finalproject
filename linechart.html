Analysis

Cancer–gene association research grew slowly through the 1980s and 1990s before experiencing a dramatic surge in the early 2000s, peaking in 2005 with over 1,200 studies published in a single year. This boom coincides with major advances in genotyping technology and the completion of the Human Genome Project in 2003, which made large-scale genetic association studies far more feasible. The sharp drop-off after 2006 likely reflects the boundaries of this dataset rather than a true decline in research activity. Across the full time range, the top genes are dominated by metabolic and detoxification enzymes, GSTM1, GSTT1, CYP1A1, and NAT2, alongside the tumor suppressor TP53. GSTM1 leads consistently throughout the boom period, reflecting sustained interest in how glutathione S-transferase variants influence cancer susceptibility across multiple cancer types. TP53 shows the longest historical presence, with activity dating back to the early 1990s, underscoring its foundational role in cancer biology research.

Zooming into the peak period of 2004–2006 reveals a shift in the research landscape: XRCC1 and ERCC2, both DNA repair genes, rise to prominence alongside GSTM1 and GSTT1, suggesting that as the field matured, attention broadened from metabolic susceptibility toward DNA damage response mechanisms. GSTT1 peaks sharply around 2004 before declining, a pattern consistent with a gene becoming well-characterized and attracting diminishing new research interest.
